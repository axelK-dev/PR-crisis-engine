# BBC Crisis Case Study: Why Speed and Structure Matter

## Background
In a Panorama documentary, the BBC edited a Trump speech in a way that implied he incited violence. A leaked internal report exposed the error, triggering resignations of the Director-General and Head of News. The BBC waited a week to issue an apology—creating a damaging vacuum that fueled political attacks and even a $1 billion lawsuit threat from Trump.

This wasn’t an isolated failure. Similar crises show the same pattern:
- **Royal Scandal:** Palace communications lagged behind social media outrage, allowing speculation to dominate headlines.
- **The Co-op vs M&S Cyberattack Responses:** The Co-op issued a transparent statement within 24 hours, calming stakeholders and earning praise for honesty. M&S delayed disclosure, facing regulatory scrutiny and reputational harm.

## Lessons Learned
- **Speed Matters:** Research shows the best PR responses occur within 24–48 hours of peak negativity. Delays allow speculation to harden into public perception.
- **Preparedness is Critical:** None of these organizations had pre-approved messaging or decision workflows. Paralysis and contradictory signals worsened the crises.
